Practice Being in the Present Moment

Miranda Johnson September 9, 2025

The practice of being present is something I return to often, both with myself and with clients. It is simple and grounded, and it brings the body and mind back into one central space — the present moment.

During meditation, I invite awareness to move through four points:

What do you hear?

What do you smell?

What do you feel?

What do you see?

These questions are not meant to change anything. They are meant to bring you back into your body and into now. When the body and mind meet in the present moment, there is clarity that cannot be forced.

There are times in life when we are constantly being called to action. We feel the need to have all the answers, or we feel overwhelmed by the thought of what is next. You do not have to be busy to be overwhelmed. You simply have to feel out of your body.

This is an exercise I use personally and with clients, especially when they begin listing all the things they must get done. It usually shows up in conversation without warning. Someone starts talking faster. Their breath gets shallow. Their mind jumps ahead, trying to solve everything at once. The pressure builds, even if nothing urgent is happening in the moment.

That is usually when I step in and say, just stop.

Not stop forever. Not ignore responsibilities. Just stop long enough to come back into the body.

"Overwhelm does not come from lack of thinking. It comes from being disconnected."

We are taught to think our way through discomfort but overwhelm does not come from lack of thinking. It comes from being disconnected. When the mind is racing ahead and the body is left behind, everything feels heavier than it actually is.

The present moment already holds what you need. It knows how you arrived here. It understands why you feel the way you feel. It is not confused by your emotions or your uncertainty. It knows how to guide you from this moment to the next, one step at a time.

When you pause and bring your attention back to what you hear, smell, feel, and see, something shifts. The nervous system begins to settle. The breath deepens without effort. The urgency softens. You are no longer trying to control what comes next. You are listening.

Stopping creates space, and space is where clarity lives. In that space, you are less likely to make decisions from fear or pressure. You begin to understand what actually needs your attention and what can wait. You notice what support is available to you, whether that is another person, a resource, or simply your own capacity to rest and breathe.

This practice is not about doing nothing. It is about doing the next right thing from a grounded place. When you allow yourself to be present, you move forward with more steadiness and fewer mistakes. You are no longer dragging yesterday or tomorrow into this moment.

The present moment is not asking you to rush. It is not demanding answers. It is asking you to arrive. When you do, you may find that what felt overwhelming begins to make sense — not all at once, but enough for the next step to reveal itself.

The present moment is not asking you to do more. It is asking you to be here.

Common Questions

What is a simple grounding exercise for when you feel overwhelmed?

Stop and ask four questions. What do you hear right now? What do you smell? What do you feel in your body? What do you see? Not to analyze the answers — just to notice them. That act of noticing pulls your mind out of what's coming and back into what is. The nervous system follows. The urgency softens.

Why does being present reduce overwhelm?

Because overwhelm is almost never about the present moment. It is about the weight of everything you are projecting forward. When the mind races ahead and the body stays behind, everything feels impossible. The present moment is the only place where the next step is visible. You cannot see it from tomorrow.

How do I stay present when I have too much to do?

You don't stay present by ignoring what needs to be done. You stay present by refusing to solve everything at once. One thing at a time is not a productivity tip — it is the only way the brain can actually function without shutting down. Stop. Breathe. Do the next right thing from a grounded place.
