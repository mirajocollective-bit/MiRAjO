# CIE Diagnostic — Family Domain Scenarios
## For Review — Do Not Load to Supabase Yet

Format: Story-based. Respondent identifies with a character, sides with someone, or decides what happens next.

Scoring key (internal only, never shown to users):
- 4 = identifies the systemic issue, asks the deeper question, or builds a structure that prevents the problem recurring
- 3 = engages but addresses the surface or stops one step short
- 2 = a legitimate instinct that has not been thought all the way through
- 1 = avoidant, dismissive, or closes the conversation down

No two options in any scenario score the same.

## BASE SCENARIOS
Everyone gets these regardless of profile.

---

### Fam1 — Who Is Holding It Together at Home

Vanessa and Derek both work in the business full time. Vanessa also handles most of what happens at home — the appointments, the groceries, the calls from school. She has not said anything directly but Derek has noticed she is tired in a way that is different. He tells himself she would say something if it was a problem.

**What is Derek missing?**

**A.** That Vanessa is waiting to be asked, not waiting to complain. `[4]`
**B.** That Vanessa's tiredness is starting to show up in her work. `[2]`
**C.** That they need to sit down and officially divide up the home responsibilities. `[3]`
**D.** Nothing. If it were serious she would have said something by now. `[1]`

---

### Fam2 — The Expansion Nobody Planned the Family For

Jerome and Patrice decided to expand their business last year. More clients, more hours, more stress. They talked about it as a business decision. They never talked about what it would mean for their evenings, their weekends, their kids' routines. Six months in the expansion is working. The family is fraying.

**What did Jerome and Patrice skip?**

**A.** They needed a family plan to go alongside the business plan. `[4]`
**B.** Patrice should have spoken up if she had concerns before they said yes. `[1]`
**C.** They needed to hire household support sooner to take the pressure off. `[2]`
**D.** They needed a 90-day check-in to see how the family was actually adjusting. `[3]`

---

### Fam3 — When the Morning Argument Follows You to Work

Nina and Andre had a disagreement before work. It was about the kids' schedule and who dropped the ball. They both said they were fine. They were not fine. By 10am it was showing up in how they were communicating in a client meeting. The client did not notice. Their team did.

**What should Nina and Andre do?**

**A.** Finish the workday and come back to it at home where it belongs. `[2]`
**B.** Take 10 minutes before the next meeting and clear enough of it to get through the day. `[3]`
**C.** Acknowledge to each other that they brought something unfinished into the day and agree on when they will finish it. `[4]`
**D.** Let it go. Every couple has bad mornings and the work still got done. `[1]`

---

### Fam4 — The Parent Who Is Always Available

Marcus and Tamara have two kids. When something comes up — sick child, school event, teacher call — it is almost always Tamara who handles it. Not because they agreed to that. It just became the pattern. Marcus tells himself she is better at it. Tamara has started to feel like she is running two full-time jobs.

**What is the real cost of this pattern?**

**A.** Tamara will burn out and when she does the business will feel it too. `[3]`
**B.** Tamara is building resentment quietly. By the time it comes out it will be bigger than any one conversation can fix. `[4]`
**C.** The kids are better off with a fully present parent even if it is just one of them. `[1]`
**D.** Marcus is missing time with his kids that he is not going to get back. `[2]`

---

### Fam5 — What You Said It Would Look Like

Before they launched the business Keisha and Raymond talked about what family life would look like. They had a plan — dinner together most nights, weekends mostly protected, the kids would not feel it. Two years in almost none of that is true. They have both adjusted without ever having a conversation about adjusting.

**What is most true about Keisha and Raymond right now?**

**A.** They are resilient. Every couple building something real goes through this phase. `[2]`
**B.** They made promises to their family they are not keeping and that has a cost even when no one is saying it out loud. `[3]`
**C.** They are operating on an outdated agreement. The plan they made does not match the life they are living and they have never updated it together. `[4]`
**D.** Raymond has given more than he bargained for and Keisha may not fully see it. `[1]`

### Fam6 — The Weeks That Change the Pace
**Condition:** family_structure = blended OR stepparent

Damon and Yvette are building a business together. Damon has two kids from his previous relationship who spend every other week with them. During those weeks the pace changes — more obligations, more pickups, more of Yvette in a role she did not fully plan for. It is working, mostly. But Damon has noticed Yvette goes a little quieter those weeks.

**What should Damon do?**

**A.** Ask Yvette directly — not if she is okay, but what those weeks actually feel like for her. `[4]`
**B.** Give her more space during those weeks. She will find her rhythm. `[1]`
**C.** Have a real conversation about what her role with his kids looks like and whether it is working for both of them. `[3]`
**D.** Let it be. She knew what she was stepping into and she has not said it is a problem. `[2]`

---

### Fam7 — Two Parents, Two Different Playbooks
**Condition:** family_structure = kids-together

Simone and David do not argue much. But when they do it is almost always about the kids. She thinks he is too lenient. He thinks she is too strict. In the business they move in sync. At home they feel like they are parenting two different children. Their son has started to figure out who to ask for what.

**What is the most important thing Simone and David need to do?**

**A.** Get on the same page about a few non-negotiables so the kids are not navigating two different households under one roof. `[4]`
**B.** Accept that they have different parenting styles and let each parent lead in their own lane. `[2]`
**C.** Have the conversation away from the house. When they are in business mode they communicate better. `[3]`
**D.** Let the kids figure it out. Different perspectives from two parents are not always a problem. `[1]`

---

### Fam8 — The Conversation They Keep Not Having
**Condition:** family_structure = no-kids OR unsure

Alicia and Trevor are two years into the business. Things are growing. The question of kids keeps coming up — not in a tense way, just in a we-have-not-really-decided way. Alicia is starting to feel the clock differently than Trevor is. He thinks there is more time. She is not sure there is.

**What does this couple actually need to talk about?**

**A.** A timeline — even a loose one — so both of them are working from the same assumption about what comes next. `[4]`
**B.** Whether they actually want kids or just think they are supposed to want them. `[3]`
**C.** Whether the business is in a place where it could absorb that kind of change. `[2]`
**D.** Nothing yet. When the time is right they will both know. `[1]`

## Notes for Miranda

- Fam1 "waiting to be asked, not waiting to complain" is the key distinction for the 4. It is about reading the person, not waiting for a signal.
- Fam6 option B scoring 1 is intentional — giving someone space when they are quietly struggling is avoidance dressed as consideration.
- Fam8 is for couples without kids yet. The 4 is about building a shared assumption, not forcing a decision.
- Notes field prompt for this domain: "Anything about your family situation you want to add? This is just for context."
